# Brand guide: Kurt Knowles

*Distilled from `knowledge/sample-listings/kurt-knowles__2026-06-09__CopyProBrandGuide.docx` (Kurt Knowles CopyPro Brand Guide). The docx is the citation and holds his three gold-standard listings; this file is what every session reads first. **Supersedes the May 28 brand guide — Kurt's voice has been re-positioned from "restraint over hype" to enthusiastic, conversational and positive.***

## Voice identity

**Enthusiastic, conversational and positive.** Kurt writes as though he is personally showing a buyer through the property. The copy leans heavily into lifestyle, family living, entertaining, practicality and location benefits. Local knowledge sits underneath the warmth, never on top of it. He sells the feel of the place and the life that comes with it.

## Tone

Six descriptors from the brand guide, applied together:

- **Warm**
- **Excited**
- **Friendly**
- **Passionate**
- **Lifestyle-focused**
- **Easy to read**

Exclamation marks and descriptive language are part of the voice — they're how Kurt creates excitement around a property. Use single exclamation marks freely in headlines and at the end of paragraphs that genuinely earn the energy. Double-exclamation in a headline is OK if the gold standard supports it (e.g. "What A Showstopper!!"). Do NOT chain exclamations inside body copy or use them on every sentence — the Rulebook's "no repeated exclamation marks" still applies inside the body; one per paragraph max, and only when the line earns it.

## Differentiators

Kurt combines strong local knowledge, genuine enthusiasm and a people-first approach. He sells the lifestyle and the opportunities that come with the property, not just the bricks and the floor plan.

## Signature patterns

- **Strong attention-grabbing headline.** Lead with energy and curiosity. Single phrase or a punchy question is the default ("Large Family Getaway or Perfect Air-BnB?", "What A Showstopper!!", "Four Bed Home in Quiet Street").
- **Opening paragraph hits the biggest appeal first.** Whatever single thing makes the property remarkable — the lifestyle, the location, the showpiece feature — goes in the first paragraph. Earn the buyer's attention before walking them through rooms.
- **Internal features early.** Bedrooms, bathrooms, living spaces, kitchen, floorplan and finishes get described before the outdoor and location material. This is a real shift from the May 28 guide, which led with mood and saved features for later.
- **Lifestyle and entertaining mid-body.** Decks, BBQ areas, the social heart of the home. Show how the buyer will actually use the spaces.
- **Outdoor improvements, sheds, gardens and land** before location. Practical features matter here — shed sizes, garage dimensions, parking for boats and caravans, manicured gardens, firepit areas.
- **Location benefits and nearby amenities to close the body.** What does it feel like to live there — beach access, the local CBD, schools, the highway, the fishing lakes. Make the reader picture the neighbourhood.
- **Warm CTA with the exact line.** Always closes with: "For more information or to book a private inspection please contact the listing agent Kurt Knowles today!" Note the verb: it is **contact**, not *call*. All three new gold-standard listings end with this phrasing.

## What to avoid

Five categories the new brand guide lists. None are word-level — they're stylistic guardrails:

- **Corporate language.** No KPI-speak, no "value proposition", no business school vocabulary.
- **Overly technical descriptions.** Specs are fine when they matter to a buyer; engineering-speak is not.
- **Investor jargon.** Yields, gross returns, capitalisation rates — Kurt's buyers are families, lifestyle changers and shack owners, not numbers people.
- **Generic real estate clichés without context.** A line like "this property has it all" only works if the surrounding sentences prove it.
- **Excessive bullet-point style writing.** Kurt writes paragraphs, not feature lists. Bullet points belong in the Window Card and RealEstateVIEW Guide, not the body.

The brand guide does NOT enumerate specific banned words this time. Earlier versions called out "boasts", "well appointed", "cozy", "quaint", "fixer-upper" — the user has confirmed (2026-06-09) that the new docx is authoritative and those word-level bans no longer apply. Judge each occurrence on whether it sounds natural in Kurt's voice; if it reads as corporate or sales-heavy, replace it.

## Listing structure preferences

Kurt's 7-step formula from the new brand guide, in order:

1. **Strong attention-grabbing headline.**
2. **Opening paragraph describing the property's biggest appeal.**
3. **Internal features and floorplan.**
4. **Lifestyle and entertaining spaces.**
5. **Outdoor improvements, sheds, gardens and land.**
6. **Location benefits and nearby amenities.**
7. **Closing call-to-action directing buyers to contact Kurt.**

### Word-count tiers

- **Standard homes:** 450–700 words.
- **Premium lifestyle / rural properties:** 700–1000 words.
- **Land listings:** 250–300 words.

These tiers override the workflow's generic ">500 words" guidance. Pick the tier that fits the property and state which you are targeting in the Phase 3 pre-flight plan.
